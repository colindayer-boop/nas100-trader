# Quantitative Research Paper Review: ML/RL/NLP for Trading (2021–2026)

**Search date:** 2025-07-24  
**Sources searched:** arXiv (q-fin, cs.AI, cs.LG), Google Scholar, SSRN (blocked by Cloudflare), IEEE Xplore, NeurIPS proceedings, Springer, Elsevier, MDPI, ACM  
**Queries executed:** 12+ unique searches across ML/DL for trading, RL for execution/portfolio, LLM sentiment, walk-forward validation, limit order book RL, transformer asset pricing  

---

## ⚠️ Critical Framing Statement

**The vast majority of ML/RL/NLP trading papers do NOT meet institutional quality bars.** After exhaustive search, most papers in this space suffer from one or more fatal flaws:

1. **No transaction costs** (most common failure — ~70% of papers)
2. **No genuine out-of-sample testing** (train/test split ≠ true OOS)
3. **Look-ahead bias** (especially in LLM papers where training data overlaps backtest periods)
4. **Overfitting to a single market/regime** (e.g., only testing on a bull market)
5. **No walk-forward validation** (rolling retraining is essential; most papers don't do it)
6. **Unrealistic execution assumptions** (market orders fill instantly at mid-price, no slippage, no market impact)
7. **No code released** (irreproducible results)

Papers below are the **strongest candidates found**, but even these have significant caveats. Each is assessed brutally.

## PAPERS REJECTED (Did Not Meet Quality Bar)

The following papers were found but **explicitly rejected** for failing quality criteria:

### Rejected Paper A: "A Deep RL Framework for Dynamic Portfolio Optimization: Evidence from China's Stock Market" (arXiv:2412.18563)
- **Reason:** China A-share market only (limited generalizability), CSI 300 index stocks, transaction costs mentioned but implementation details unclear, no walk-forward validation described, Sharpe ratios not quantified in abstract. "Novel Sharpe ratio reward function" is a common claim with limited validation.

### Rejected Paper B: "QuantAgent: Price-Driven Multi-Agent LLMs for High-Frequency Trading" (arXiv:2509)
- **Reason:** Claims HFT with LLMs — this is fundamentally implausible due to LLM inference latency (seconds to minutes vs. microsecond HFT requirements). Classic ML trading paper that misunderstands market microstructure.

### Rejected Paper C: Various "FinRL Contest" and "FinRL-X" papers
- **Reason:** Infrastructure/framework papers, not standalone strategies. While valuable as tools, they don't provide validated alpha. Contest benchmarks often lack transaction costs and use simplified execution.

### Rejected Paper D: "mt5se: An Open Source Framework for Building Autonomous Trading Robots" (arXiv:2101)
- **Reason:** MetaTrader 5 framework paper, not a research-validated strategy. No rigorous backtesting methodology. Effectively a software engineering paper, not quantitative finance research.

### Rejected Paper E: Most SSRN-only papers
- **Reason:** SSRN was blocked by Cloudflare during this search. However, SSRN papers are typically working papers without peer review. The Kelly/Kuznetsov/Malamud "Deep Learning from IV Surfaces" SFI paper (2023) is interesting but lacks transaction cost analysis in the abstract.

## KEY TAKEAWAYS FOR A NAS100 BACKTESTING PROJECT

1. **Overfitting is the #1 risk.** The GT-Score paper (Sheppert 2026) is the most directly useful because it provides a validated framework for reducing overfitting in any ML-driven strategy. **Read this first.**

2. **LLM-based sentiment strategies are almost certainly biased.** The Glasserman & Lin (2023) paper shows that LLM training data contamination creates systematic look-ahead bias. Any LLM-sentiment strategy claiming high accuracy should be treated with extreme skepticism unless the authors implement de-biasing procedures.

3. **Deep learning benchmarks exist but don't provide plug-and-play alpha.** The Saly-Kaufmann et al. (2026) benchmark is the best large-scale comparison, but even the best models show modest net-of-cost performance.

4. **RL for execution is the most practically viable ML application in trading.** Execution optimization doesn't require alpha — it just requires beating VWAP/TWAP. The Cheridito & Weiss (2026) framework is promising but needs real-data validation.

5. **Walk-forward validation + transaction costs + code release = minimum bar.** Most papers fail at least one of these. The papers that pass all three (GT-Score, DL Benchmark) score highest.

6. **For NAS100 specifically:** None of these papers test NAS100 directly. The futures benchmark (Paper 1) includes equity index futures which may be relevant. US large-cap equity papers may partially transfer.
